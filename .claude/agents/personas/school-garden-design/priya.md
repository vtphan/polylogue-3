## What You Know

Winters here kill most warm-season crops, and summer break means 10 to 12 weeks where nobody is around to take care of the garden. You can explain clearly why both of those problems matter — winter kills the plants, and summer absence means even the tough ones go untended. This is a real insight, and you can make a strong case for why any school garden needs to deal with the season problem.

You also learned a lot from your field trip about what greenhouses can do. You saw temperature-controlled rooms, automated watering systems, and grow lights. You can describe what you saw and explain why each feature matters for plant growth — extending the growing season, protecting from weather extremes, growing crops that wouldn't survive outdoors in Memphis. You came away genuinely excited about the possibilities.

You use the phrase "controlled environment" confidently, but the systems behind it are fuzzy to you. You know the university greenhouse had ventilation fans and thermostats, but you haven't thought about who monitors and adjusts those systems every day, or what it costs to heat a greenhouse through a Memphis winter, or what happens if the power goes out in February. When you say "controlled," you're thinking of it as something that mostly takes care of itself once you set it up.

You also noticed that the university greenhouse was big and well-equipped, and you figure a smaller version for a school would just cost less and work proportionally as well. You haven't thought about the fact that a university has dedicated greenhouse staff, institutional maintenance budgets, and graduate students providing free labor — things a middle school doesn't have.

You haven't considered who would actually run the greenhouse day-to-day. The question of who checks on it every day, including weekends and holidays, or who that person would be at a middle school, just hasn't occurred to you. You also haven't thought about energy costs — heating in winter, ventilation in Memphis's hot summers, electricity for automated systems. You've been focused on the structure, not the operating costs.

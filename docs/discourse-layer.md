# Polylogue 3: Discourse Layer

How group presentations and group discussions are structured, and how roles work within them. See [Nomenclature](nomenclature.md) for consistent vocabulary.

---

## Design Goal

The discourse layer specifies how AI agents interact in two activities that mirror PBL practice at University Middle School: **group presentations** and **group discussions**. Both activities are designed to produce discourse containing identifiable critical thinking flaws that students can practice evaluating.

This document does not specify what agents *are* (see [Agent Architecture](agent-architecture.md)) or how discourse is *evaluated* (see evaluation layer, TBD).

### Guiding Principles

**Rethought for PBL, not retrofitted from Polylogue 2.** Polylogue 2's discourse layer modeled multi-party conversation with seven phases, five discourse types, and a selector mechanism. That design served a different purpose — generating rich conversations where fallacies emerge from interaction dynamics. Polylogue 3's discourse layer serves PBL: it needs to produce the kinds of presentations and discussions that middle school students actually encounter and need to critique.

**Two activities, different mechanics.** Presentation and discussion are structurally different activities. Presentations are planned, linear, and role-structured. Discussions are reactive, non-linear, and dynamic. They share agents but not much else.

**LLM-generated, human-curated.** Like agent definitions, discourse outputs are generated by LLMs. The discourse layer specifies enough structure for an LLM to produce realistic, flaw-containing output — but not so much that it becomes a rigid script.

## Presentation

### What a Group Presentation Is

A team of agents delivers a prepared presentation on a PBL topic. The team has (supposedly) researched, collaborated, and built a product. The presentation is the public-facing result of that work.

This mirrors the STRIPES Showcase at UMS, where student teams present their projects to an authentic audience.

### Presentation Sections

A presentation moves through sections. Unlike discussion phases, sections are **planned and linear** — the team has decided the structure in advance.

| Section | Purpose | What happens |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Introduction** | Frame the problem and the team's angle | State the driving question, explain why it matters, preview what the team did |
| **Approach** | Explain what the team did or proposes | Describe the research process, the design choices, the methods |
| **Findings / Evidence** | Present supporting material | Data, research results, examples, prototype demonstration |
| **Solution / Proposal** | State what the team recommends or built | The product, the plan, the design — the team's answer to the driving question |
| **Conclusion** | Wrap up and reflect | Summarize, discuss limitations, suggest next steps, connect back to the driving question |

Not every presentation needs all five sections. A simpler project might combine Approach and Findings. A more developed one might split Findings into multiple parts. The sections are a default structure, not a rigid template.

### Where Flaws Live in Presentations

Different sections are natural homes for different flaw types:

| Section | Common flaw types | Example |
|---------|-------------------|---------|
| Introduction | Framing too narrow or too broad; unstated assumptions about the problem | "Water pollution is the biggest environmental threat" (overstated, unqualified) |
| Approach | Gaps in methodology; unjustified choices | "We decided to focus on one river" (why? no rationale given) |
| Findings | Overstated evidence; selective data; correlation treated as causation | "Pollution went down after the cleanup, so the cleanup worked" (other factors?) |
| Solution | Missing feasibility; unaddressed tradeoffs; incomplete stakeholder analysis | "The community should install filters on every drain" (cost? who pays? maintenance?) |
| Conclusion | Claims that go beyond what the evidence supports; ignoring limitations | "Our solution will solve water pollution in Memphis" (from one river study?) |

This mapping helps with topic-first design: if you want students to practice identifying feasibility gaps, weight the scenario toward the Solution section.

### Roles in Presentations

Roles are assigned at the scenario level, not built into agent identity (see [Agent Architecture](agent-architecture.md), "Roles Are Not Part of the Agent Definition"). The same project roles that define what each agent worked on naturally determine which sections they present — the agent who did the research presents the findings, the agent who designed the solution presents the proposal.

Roles are flexible and scenario-specific. The scenario designer (teacher or LLM) defines whatever roles fit the project. At UMS, real PBL teams use roles like prototype design engineer, operations engineer, and product design engineer. A Polylogue 3 scenario might use similar roles, or simpler ones like Researcher, Designer, and Community Liaison — whatever suits the project context. The architecture imposes no fixed role set.

**Pedagogically productive mismatch:** The interesting cases are when an agent's knowledge profile doesn't match their role well. A Researcher with shallow understanding of the science. A Designer with a blind spot on feasibility. These mismatches produce natural, recognizable flaws.

### Turn Structure in Presentations

Presentations have a **planned turn order**, not a dynamic one. The role assignment determines who speaks when. Within each section, the responsible agent delivers their part. Other agents may contribute briefly (a handoff, a supporting point), but the primary speaker is predetermined.

This means there is no "selector" mechanism for presentations. The role-to-section mapping is determined at scenario creation — each section is assigned to the agent whose role makes them the natural presenter for that content. Transitions between sections happen through natural handoffs between speakers.

### Coordination as a Flaw Source

One flaw type that's unique to team presentations: **coordination gaps**. These happen when different team members' contributions don't fit together — the evidence doesn't actually support the proposed solution, or the introduction frames a problem that the rest of the presentation doesn't address, or two speakers contradict each other.

Coordination gaps emerge naturally when agents have different knowledge profiles. If the Researcher knows the science well but the Designer has a misconception about how the science applies to their solution, the presentation will have an internal disconnect that a careful evaluator can identify.

## Discussion

### What a Group Discussion Is

A group of agents discusses a PBL topic — working through ideas, reacting to each other's contributions, building or challenging arguments. Unlike a presentation, the discussion is not planned in advance. It unfolds in real time.

This mirrors the Develop/Critique phase at UMS, where students work in groups: brainstorming, giving feedback, identifying problems, and working toward decisions about their project.

### Discussion Flow

Polylogue 2 defined seven phases with specific transition rules. For Polylogue 3, we simplify to describe **how PBL discussions actually move**, rather than modeling conversational dynamics in the abstract.

A PBL group discussion typically moves through three broad stages:

| Stage | What's happening | Character |
|-------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Opening up** | Sharing initial ideas, putting things on the table, asking questions | Generative, exploratory, multiple directions at once |
| **Working through** | Building on ideas, challenging claims, identifying problems, going deeper on specific threads | Focused, reactive, sometimes tense — this is where most content happens |
| **Converging** | Moving toward decisions, synthesizing, wrapping up | Narrowing, resolving (or failing to resolve), summarizing |

These stages are looser than Polylogue 2's phases. They describe the general arc of a productive (or unproductive) group discussion without prescribing specific transitions. A discussion might loop between Opening up and Working through multiple times. It might never reach Converging. It might jump straight to Working through if the topic is already familiar.

**Why simplify from Polylogue 2's seven phases?** The seven-phase model (opening, exploratory, deepening, assertive, tension, synthesis, closing) was designed to support fine-grained phase-dependent evaluation — the same utterance judged differently in exploratory vs. assertive phase. That's a powerful idea, but it requires the system to reliably detect which of seven phases the conversation is in, and it adds complexity that may not serve the PBL context well. Three stages are easier for LLMs to track and for humans to reason about, while still capturing the meaningful arc of a group discussion.

### Where Flaws Live in Discussions

In discussions, flaws come from **two sources** (see brainstorm document, "Core Insight: Two Sources of Flaws"):

**Knowledge-driven flaws** are present from the start — agents bring their misconceptions, shallow understanding, and blind spots into the discussion. These surface whenever an agent speaks about something they don't fully understand.

**Interaction-driven flaws** emerge from the dynamics of the exchange:

| Dynamic | What happens | Flaw type |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Challenge → escalation** | Agent is questioned, responds by doubling down rather than engaging | Overstatement, false certainty |
| **Challenge → deflection** | Agent is questioned, changes the subject or appeals to something irrelevant | Red herring, appeal to authority |
| **Social pressure → conformity** | Agent abandons a valid point because the group isn't receptive | Loss of important perspective (a group-level flaw) |
| **Enthusiasm → steamrolling** | Agent dominates the discussion, other perspectives get crowded out | Incomplete analysis, echo chamber |
| **Superficial agreement** | Agents agree without actually resolving underlying disagreements | False consensus, unexamined assumptions |

These interaction-driven flaws are what make discussion evaluation more challenging than presentation evaluation — they require students to track not just *what* was said but *why* and *in response to what*.

### Roles in Discussions

Discussion roles are optional. A discussion can be **unstructured** (agents participate freely, as in a natural group conversation) or **role-structured** (agents are assigned roles that shape their participation).

**Unstructured discussion** is the default and more closely mirrors how student groups actually work during the Develop/Critique phase — messy, organic, driven by whoever has something to say. Agents carry their project roles from the scenario, which influence what they know and care about, but no formal discussion role constrains how they participate.

**Role-structured discussion** assigns participation roles that shape (but don't fully determine) what each agent does. The architecture supports any role framework — the scenario designer chooses whatever roles suit the pedagogical intent. Two examples:

**Project-based roles.** Agents carry their project roles (e.g., Researcher, Designer, Community Liaison) into the discussion. Each agent contributes from the perspective of their project responsibility. This mirrors how real PBL teams discuss — the person who did the research speaks to the evidence, the designer defends the proposal.

**Six Thinking Hats.** Agents are assigned de Bono's thinking hats — White (facts/data), Red (feelings/intuitions), Black (caution/risks), Yellow (benefits/optimism), Green (creative alternatives), Blue (process/overview). Each agent is expected to contribute from their hat's perspective. This creates a structured discussion where students can evaluate whether agents fulfill or violate their assigned thinking role — a Black Hat agent with a blind spot on feasibility who fails to identify the biggest risk, or a White Hat agent with shallow understanding who presents vague claims as solid data.

Any role framework works because the `role` field in the scenario is a free-form string, not a constrained set. The agent's disposition and knowledge profile determine *how* they fulfill their role (and where they fall short).

Role-structured discussions are useful for pedagogical scaffolding: students first evaluate role-structured discussions (clearer expectations, easier to identify when a role is performed poorly), then unstructured ones (more realistic, more complex). Or the reverse — the ordering is a pedagogical choice, not an architectural one.

### Turn-Taking in Discussions

Unlike presentations, discussions need a mechanism for determining who speaks next. Options:

| Approach | How it works | When to use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Responsive** | Next speaker is whoever has the most relevant response to what was just said | Most natural; produces realistic flow |
| **Round robin** | Fixed rotation | Simple; ensures equal participation |
| **Facilitated** | One agent acts as moderator, directing the conversation | Models structured classroom discussion |

The default should be **responsive** — it produces the most realistic group dynamics and the most interesting interaction-driven flaws.

## Shared Concerns

### How Many Agents?

The number of agents is determined by the project context — specifically, what roles the project naturally requires. The LLM identifies appropriate roles when generating a scenario, and the teacher can revise. PBL groups at UMS typically have 3–5 members (groups of about 5 in the 7th grade project, for instance), which serves as a natural starting point, but the architecture imposes no fixed count.

### Length and Scope

Presentations should be **focused and relatively short** — long enough to contain meaningful content and identifiable flaws, short enough for students to analyze carefully. Think: a 5-10 minute student presentation, rendered as text.

Discussions should be **long enough for dynamics to develop** — interaction-driven flaws need time to emerge. But not so long that evaluation becomes overwhelming. Think: a 15-20 minute group work session, rendered as text.

Specific turn counts or word limits are implementation details, not architecture.

### Connecting to the Agent Layer

Both activities draw on the same agent definitions (context + knowledge profile + disposition). The key difference is which components drive flaw generation:

| Component | Presentation | Discussion |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Context | Shapes the topic and standards | Same |
| Knowledge profile | **Primary flaw driver** — gaps, misconceptions, blind spots are embedded in the prepared content | **Active flaw driver** — same as presentation, plus knowledge gaps are exposed by questioning |
| Disposition | Shapes how flaws are *expressed* (confidently, vaguely, emotionally) | **Active flaw driver** — dispositions + reactive tendencies produce interaction-driven flaws |

## Relationship to Polylogue 2's Discourse Layer

| Aspect | Polylogue 2 | Polylogue 3 | Rationale |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Activities | One (conversation) | Two (presentation, discussion) | PBL involves both activities |
| Phases | 7 phases with transition rules | Presentation: 5 sections (linear). Discussion: 3 stages (loose) | Simpler; aligned to PBL practice |
| Discourse types | 5 (inquiry, deliberation, debate, support, casual) | Not used — activities replace types | PBL contexts don't map cleanly to abstract discourse types |
| Turn-taking | Selector mechanism | Presentation: planned. Discussion: responsive (default) | Different activities need different structures |
| Roles | Discussant + Selector | Scenario-defined, flexible. Presentation: project roles determine section assignments. Discussion: unstructured (default) or role-structured (any framework, including Six Thinking Hats) | Roles assigned at scenario level, not agent identity |
| Context parameters | stakes, education_level, discourse_type | Handled by agent context | Agent context already encodes "middle school student working on X" |

**What carries over:** The idea that structure shapes what flaws look like and where they surface. The principle that dynamics should emerge from agent characteristics, not be scripted. The notion that different contexts produce different evaluative standards.

**What changes:** Everything specific to conversational dynamics is rethought for PBL. The phase system is replaced by structures that match actual PBL activities. Discourse types are replaced by the two activities. Roles move from system-level (discussant/selector) to activity-level (presentation roles, discussion roles).

## Q&A Is a Pedagogical Activity, Not a Discourse Mode

Q&A after a presentation is not a third activity type in the architecture. It is a **student-driven activity** that uses the AI-generated presentation as input.

The approach: AI generates presentations (and discussions) in advance as prepared artifacts. Students view the presentation, identify flaws, and then engage in Q&A — but the Q&A is conducted by students, not by AI agents. Students can play both roles: formulating questions that probe the flaws they've identified, and role-playing how a flawed presenter would respond.

This is pedagogically richer than having AI handle Q&A. When students formulate probing questions, they're exercising critical thinking actively — not just identifying flaws but articulating *why* something is a flaw and *what information would expose it*. When they role-play answers, they internalize the relationship between knowledge gaps and defensive or evasive responses.

Real-time AI Q&A (where students ask and AI agents respond live) is a possible future extension but involves technical complexity beyond the current scope. The student-driven approach is a better starting point — it's simpler architecturally and stronger pedagogically.

## Open Questions

1. **[Resolved] How structured should discussion stages be?** Three stages with conservative transition detection, implemented in the stage-tracker subagent. The stage-tracker monitors for transition signals and requires 2–3 turns of evidence before declaring a transition. Stages can loop (back to opening_up) and may skip converging. See `configs/discussion/subagents/stage-tracker.md`.

2. **[Resolved] Role assignment mechanism.** The LLM suggests roles based on the project context when generating a scenario; the teacher reviews and revises at the human curation checkpoint. Roles are flexible and scenario-specific — the architecture imposes no fixed role set. See the Roles sections above and `configs/scenario/subagents/scenario-generator.md`.

3. **Facilitated vs. unfacilitated discussion.** Should one agent sometimes act as a discussion facilitator (mirroring a teacher's role in structuring group work)? This adds realism but also adds a special-purpose agent type.

4. **Multiple discussions on the same topic.** In PBL, groups revise and re-discuss after feedback. Could Polylogue 3 model a sequence: presentation → feedback → revised discussion → revised presentation? This mirrors the UMS Develop/Critique cycle but adds significant complexity.
